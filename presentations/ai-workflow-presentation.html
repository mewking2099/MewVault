Switching modes of work costs more than we admit.

What If Your Workflow Was a Product?

I had been designing for other people's problems. I had never designed for mine.

  • Constraint: it had to get smarter over time, not just sit there

Act II — The Design Thinking Moment

Most of us know our workflow frustrations. Very few of us have written them down.

  • I listed every moment in my week that felt unnecessarily hard

Act II — The Design Thinking Moment

Act IV — How It Learns and Grows

A Quick Vocabulary

Eight words you will hear — in plain English.

Context Window The AI's working memory for a session. Think of it as a whiteboard — it can only hold so much before old things get erased.
Context Injection Loading the right information before work begins — project rules, past decisions, your writing style — so the AI starts informed, not blank.
Compacting Shrinking a long conversation into a shorter summary so the whiteboard doesn't fill up. The AI keeps working without losing what matters.
Prompt Caching Reusing the cost of sending the same instructions twice. If the rules haven't changed, you don't pay to re-explain them every session.
Hooks Automatic checks that run before or after the AI does something — like a spell-checker that fires every time a file is saved.
Model Routing Sending different tasks to different AI models. Simple jobs go to cheaper, faster models. Complex thinking goes to smarter ones.
Agents Specialised AI workers with one job each. Instead of one AI doing everything, you spin up a team — a researcher, a coder, a reviewer.
MCP A standard plug socket for connecting tools to the AI — Figma, GitHub, your calendar. One protocol, any service.

The Silos · software-projects

Stop Building the Wrong Thing

Problem

Features grew beyond what anyone agreed on. Rework was expensive. Progress felt high but direction kept shifting. Decisions made in conversation were forgotten by the next session.

Solution

Three tiers of planning. Small task: start immediately. Medium: agree on the approach first, then build. Large or risky: write a formal plan with phases, risks, and a way to undo it — nothing is built until that plan is approved.

Test

Every large change has a traceable plan that was approved before work began. If the work drifts from it, there is a written record of where it diverged and why.

The Silos · idea-hub

A Pipeline, Not a Graveyard

Problem

Good ideas and bad ideas looked identical sitting in a notes app. No way to tell them apart without spending hours on research — which made it easier to just not look at them at all.

Solution

Capture fast, filter early. An idea needs a problem statement and an identified user before any research time is spent. Validation then runs a structured workflow — market scan, competitors, effort estimate — and produces a sourced document you can make a real decision from.

Test

Every idea has a visible lifecycle status. It either moves forward with evidence or gets archived with a reason. Nothing just sits there rotting. The pipeline is the accountability.

The Silos · game-lab

One Step at a Time, Every Time

Problem

Creative sessions got bloated. Too many things changed at once, too hard to isolate what broke, too easy to end a session with "I think it works" as the only measure of progress.

Solution

One change per step, no exceptions. Every step ends with a specific thing to run and a specific thing to look for. If something breaks, everything stops until it is fixed — no building on top of a broken foundation.

Test

Every mechanic built and every concept learned is logged with a count. The session only closes when the log is updated and the next step is defined. "I think it works" is not how a session ends.

The Silos · learn-lab

Consistency Over Intensity

Problem

Daily practice lives or dies on showing up. But relying on motivation alone does not work — and using AI for every single repetition is both expensive and inconsistent.

Solution

Split the job between two tools. A simple script handles the scheduling — the same way every time, at no cost. The AI handles the human part: conversation, explanation, examples. Each does only what it is best at.

Test

One command shows reps completed, accuracy rate, current streak, and what is due tomorrow — all computed locally, no AI needed. Practice either happened or it did not. The number is honest.

Under the Hood

The Stack

Claude Code The AI you talk to. It reads files, writes code, runs commands, and works alongside you like a co-worker who never forgets the codebase.
Impeccable A quality checker for UI work. Before any design file is saved, it catches bad patterns — like text too small to read or colours that don't contrast enough.
Graphify A map of the entire codebase. Instead of reading every file to find something, the AI consults this map first — faster and cheaper.
LiteLLM A traffic controller for AI models. Sends simple tasks to cheaper models and complex thinking to smarter ones — automatically.
Figma MCP A direct line between the AI and your Figma files. It reads measurements, components, and decisions so you never have to copy them over manually.
Obsidian A notebook that updates itself. Every project's status, decisions, and logs flow here automatically after each session — you just browse it.
Doobidoo Long-term memory for the vault. Stores decisions, lessons, and gotchas so they surface again when they're relevant — even months later.
Ollama An AI that runs entirely on your own machine. Used for searching and indexing notes — free to run, no internet, no data leaving your laptop.
Hooks Automatic rules that run silently in the background. They catch mistakes — like saving a password or skipping a plan — before they happen.
SM-2 / srs.py A learning scheduler. Figures out the perfect moment to review each flashcard based on how well you remembered it last time.
Syncthing A sync tool that works without the cloud. Notes written on your phone appear on your laptop automatically, peer-to-peer.
mew CLI The control panel for the whole vault. One short command and it figures out which silo to work in, which AI to use, and what to do.

  • Name the friction — specific, not vague. Not "I hate handoffs." Something like "writing a project summary always takes me two hours and still feels shallow."

  • Start small — one rule, one template, one silo. The smallest version that removes the friction is the right starting point.
