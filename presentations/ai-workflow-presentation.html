Act I — The Problem

Ten Tools, Zero Memory

Your best work is scattered across every app you have ever opened.

  • Figma for design, Notion for notes, Slack for decisions, email for feedback
  • Each tool holds a fragment — none of them talk to each other
  • When a project ends, most of what you learned disappears with it
  • You are not disorganised. The system you inherited is.
Act I — The Problem

AI Helped With Tasks.
Not With Me.

Every AI tool I tried was smart in a vacuum and useless in context.

  • "Write me a case study" — it had no idea what decisions I actually made
  • "Review this design" — it had no idea what standards my team cared about
  • I was re-explaining my world from scratch, every single session
  • Great at answering questions. Could not hold my workflow together.
Act I — The Problem

The Context-Switching Tax

Switching modes of work costs more than we admit.

  • Deep design work, career admin, learning, side projects — all need different headspace
  • Every interruption means re-loading context into your own brain
  • The tax is invisible, so we never design around it
  • I kept feeling behind without being able to say why
Act II — The Design Thinking Moment

What If Your Workflow Was a Product?

I had been designing for other people's problems. I had never designed for mine.

  • User research subject: me, on a bad Monday
  • The brief: reduce the cost of switching between modes of work
  • Constraint: it had to get smarter over time, not just sit there
  • Insight: I did not need one better tool. I needed focused tools for focused modes.
Act II — The Design Thinking Moment

Empathise With Yourself

Most of us know our workflow frustrations. Very few of us have written them down.

  • I listed every moment in my week that felt unnecessarily hard
  • Patterns emerged: case studies, skill tracking, decisions with no paper trail
  • The pain was not the work itself — it was the invisible overhead around the work
  • That overhead is a design problem. It has a solution.
Act II — The Design Thinking Moment

The Insight:
One Job Per Workspace

A general workspace is no one's workspace.

  • Each mode of work has its own rhythm, vocabulary, and definition of done
  • A silo is a focused space with rules the AI follows inside it
  • Different standards, different templates, different behaviour — same AI
  • Think of it as giving the AI a job description, not just a prompt

Act IV — How It Learns and Grows

A System That Gets Smarter

Most tools stay the same no matter how long you use them. This one does not.

  • At the end of each session, useful corrections become rules
  • Next session, those rules are loaded before work begins
  • The AI does not repeat mistakes I have already caught
  • It is not memory in a chatbot sense — it is designed behaviour, extracted from real use
Act IV — How It Learns and Grows

The Planning Gate:
Safety by Design

I needed the AI to stop me from moving too fast on the wrong things.

  • Small tasks: start immediately — no friction where none is needed
  • Medium tasks: propose the approach in conversation, wait for a nod
  • Big changes: written plan, reviewed and approved before anything is built
  • The gate is enforced automatically — the system cannot be bypassed even when you want to rush

You need to find your one friction point and design the response to it.

  • What is the part of your week that costs the most invisible energy?
  • Where do you lose work that you should have kept?
  • Where does AI help you with a task but not with your actual problem?
  • That is your design brief. You are both the designer and the user.

What a Silo
Actually Is

A silo is just a focused workspace — and you already know how to design one.

  • One clear job to be done
  • Rules for how the AI should behave inside this context

  • A definition of done, so you know when it is working

The Material Is New.
The Method Is Not.

You have been solving problems for users your whole career. This time the user is you.

  • AI is a material — like type, colour, or layout — with properties you can design around
  • Design thinking is still the method: empathise, define, prototype, test
  • The prototype does not have to be MewVault — it can be one rule in one workspace
  • Start with the smallest version of the solution. Sound familiar?

The Silos in Practice

Six focused workspaces. Each one started with a real problem. Each one has a definition of done.

The Silos · career-studio

Your Best Work Keeps Disappearing

Problem

Every time you update your CV or write a case study, you reconstruct your own work from memory. The real decisions — the trade-offs, the rationale, the things that made the project interesting — are gone the moment the project closes.

Solution

Every working session quietly logs a receipt: what was decided, why, and what it looked like. When you need a case study, it assembles a first draft from those receipts. You edit it to sound like yourself — and each edit teaches it your voice for next time.

Test

A skill matrix with five pillars tracks your actual growth. Every skill level needs an evidence link — no file, no level. Mock interviews pull from your real project history, not generic questions.

The Silos · design-studio

Decisions That Actually Stay

Problem

Every design session started from zero. Measurements read by hand from Figma. Last week's reasoning lost in a thread. No paper trail between the design choice and the outcome.

Solution

The workspace reads Figma directly — no manual transcription. Every decision is saved with the frame that informed it, the constraint that shaped it, and the score it received. The next session loads only what is relevant to today's work.

Test

One sync command flows every design decision automatically into the shared knowledge vault, linked to its Figma source. Nothing is lost between sessions. Anyone can trace a decision back to where it came from.

The Silos · software-projects

Stop Building the Wrong Thing

Problem

Features grew beyond what anyone agreed on. Rework was expensive. Progress felt high but direction kept shifting. Decisions made in conversation were forgotten by the next session.

Solution

Three tiers of planning. Small task: start immediately. Medium: agree on the approach first, then build. Large or risky: write a formal plan with phases, risks, and a way to undo it — nothing is built until that plan is approved.

Test

Every large change has a traceable plan that was approved before work began. If the work drifts from it, there is a written record of where it diverged and why.

The Silos · idea-hub

A Pipeline, Not a Graveyard

Problem

Good ideas and bad ideas looked identical sitting in a notes app. No way to tell them apart without spending hours on research — which made it easier to just not look at them at all.

Solution

Capture fast, filter early. An idea needs a problem statement and an identified user before any research time is spent. Validation then runs a structured workflow — market scan, competitors, effort estimate — and produces a sourced document you can make a real decision from.

Test

Every idea has a visible lifecycle status. It either moves forward with evidence or gets archived with a reason. Nothing just sits there rotting. The pipeline is the accountability.

The Silos · game-lab

One Step at a Time, Every Time

Problem

Creative sessions got bloated. Too many things changed at once, too hard to isolate what broke, too easy to end a session with "I think it works" as the only measure of progress.

Solution

One change per step, no exceptions. Every step ends with a specific thing to run and a specific thing to look for. If something breaks, everything stops until it is fixed — no building on top of a broken foundation.

Test

Every mechanic built and every concept learned is logged with a count. The session only closes when the log is updated and the next step is defined. "I think it works" is not how a session ends.

The Silos · learn-lab

Consistency Over Intensity

Problem

Daily practice lives or dies on showing up. But relying on motivation alone does not work — and using AI for every single repetition is both expensive and inconsistent.

Solution

Split the job between two tools. A simple script handles the scheduling — the same way every time, at no cost. The AI handles the human part: conversation, explanation, examples. Each does only what it is best at.

Test

One command shows reps completed, accuracy rate, current streak, and what is due tomorrow — all computed locally, no AI needed. Practice either happened or it did not. The number is honest.
