# Speaker Notes — Building for Yourself
> Quick study guide. One section per slide. Bold = the one thing to land. Plain text = what to say or remember.

---

## Slide 1 — Title: Building for Yourself

- Open with a pause. Let the title sit.
- **This talk is about a problem I had, not a tool I built.** The tool came second.
- One sentence framing: "I applied design thinking to my own workflow — and I want to show you what that looks like and what it unlocked."
- Tone-setter: warm, peer-to-peer. Not a pitch, not a tutorial.

---

## Act I — The Problem

### Slide 2 — Ten Tools, Zero Memory

- **The problem is not you. The system you inherited is broken.**
- Everyone in the room can name their own version of this list: Figma, Notion, Slack, email, Google Drive, voice notes.
- The insight: each tool was designed for ONE job. Nobody designed the space between them.
- Ask the room (rhetorically): "When was the last time you tried to write a case study and couldn't remember why you made a decision you made six months ago?"

---

### Slide 3 — AI Helped With Tasks. Not With Me.

- **The problem with AI tools is that they have no memory of you.**
- Every session starts from zero. You paste context. You re-explain the project. You re-explain your standards.
- The AI is smart. But it doesn't know you. That's the gap.
- Personal note: "I kept trying different AI tools and they kept impressing me in demos and frustrating me in real work."
- Transition: "So I started thinking about this differently."

---

### Slide 4 — The Context-Switching Tax

- **This is the invisible cost nobody talks about.**
- It's not just time — it's mental mode. Switching from deep design work to career admin to a side project isn't free.
- Each switch costs a reload: where was I, what were the constraints, what did I last decide?
- The tax is invisible because it doesn't show up on a calendar. It shows up as feeling behind for no clear reason.
- This slide is validation — let the room nod before you move on.

---

## Act II — The Design Thinking Moment

### Slide 5 — What If Your Workflow Was a Product?

- **This is the reframe. Everything else follows from it.**
- We design for users every day. We think about friction, flow, definition of done. We almost never do that for ourselves.
- "I did a bit of user research. The user was me, on a bad Monday."
- The brief I wrote: "Reduce the cost of switching between modes of work. It has to get smarter over time — not just sit there."
- This is design thinking applied inward. That's the whole talk in one slide.

---

### Slide 6 — Empathise With Yourself

- **Write your frustrations down. The pattern is already there.**
- I made a list. Every moment in the week that felt unnecessarily hard. Not vague — specific. "Writing a case study takes me a whole day and it's always worse than it should be."
- The patterns I found: case studies, skill tracking, design decisions with no paper trail, ideas that died in notes.
- The pain wasn't the work — it was the overhead around the work. That overhead is designable.
- Invite the room: "What would be on your list?"

---

### Slide 7 — The Insight: One Job Per Workspace

- **A general workspace is no one's workspace.**
- The key idea: instead of one tool that does everything badly, design focused spaces that each do one thing well — with the AI behaving appropriately for each one.
- Think of it like giving the AI a job description. In the design space, it follows design standards. In the career space, it knows your voice and your history.
- Same AI. Different job descriptions. Totally different outputs.
- Transition: "Here's what that looks like in practice."

---

## Act III — MewVault as a Case Study

### Slide 8 — The Case Study Problem I Could Not Ignore

- **The work you do is richer than the story you tell about it.**
- The problem is universal for designers: the real decisions — the Figma rationale, the trade-offs, the "we considered X but chose Y because Z" — live in your head and then disappear.
- CV time becomes archaeology. You dig through old files, old Slack threads, trying to reconstruct what you actually did.
- "The version I ended up writing was always shallower than the project deserved."
- I needed the system to collect evidence while I worked, automatically.

---

### Slide 9 — Receipts First, Case Study Second

- **Every working session now quietly logs what happened.**
- The word "receipts" is intentional. Think of it like expense tracking — you don't reconstruct your receipts after a trip, you collect them as you go.
- When I say "case study GoldDiamond," the system assembles a first draft from those receipts. It knows which Figma frames were referenced, which decisions were made, what the audit scores were.
- Then I do a voice pass — I edit it to sound like me. Each edit teaches the system my writing style.
- The next draft needs less correction. It gets closer to my voice over time.

---

### Slide 10 — The Skill Matrix That Tells the Truth

- **"Where do you want to be in 5 years?" deserves a better answer than a feeling.**
- Five pillars: Design, Product, Development, AI & Tooling, Leadership.
- The rule is simple and ruthless: every skill level needs an evidence link. Not self-assessment. A file. A case study. A project receipt. No file, no level.
- This matters because it catches the drift — you can feel like you're growing in Leadership while the matrix shows no evidence in 40 days.
- Mock interviews pull from real vault history. "Walk me through the GoldDiamond dropdown decision" — that's a real question from a real file, not a generic behavioural template.

---

### Slide 11 — The Idea Graveyard

- **Every designer has a list of ideas that never moved. I had one too.**
- The problem: in a flat list, a terrible idea and a brilliant idea look identical. You can't tell them apart without spending hours on research.
- So the easy answer is: don't look at the list. And that's what I was doing.
- idea-hub adds a triage gate. A seed needs a problem statement and a real user before any research time is spent. That filter kills weak ideas fast and cheaply.
- Good ideas then go through a structured validation: market scan, competitors, effort estimate. Evidence-based, not gut-based.

---

### Slide 12 — Before and After

- **The work didn't change. The overhead did.**
- Before: every session started with reconstruction. What was the context? What did I last decide? What files do I need?
- After: the system holds that. It knows which project it's in, what standards apply, what I corrected last time.
- The result: I spend more time in the work and less time managing the work.
- This is the promise — not AI doing your job. AI removing the friction around your job so you can do it better.

## Act IV — How It Learns and Grows

### Slide 13 — A System That Gets Smarter

- **Most tools stay the same no matter how long you use them. This one learns.**
- At the end of each working session, I review what happened. Any corrections I made, any preferences I expressed — those get extracted as rules.
- Next session, those rules are loaded at the start. The AI doesn't repeat mistakes I've already caught.
- Example: I once corrected it on how I like case studies to open. That correction is now a rule. It hasn't made that mistake since.
- This is not chatbot memory. It's designed behaviour — written, versioned, explicit.

---

### Slide 14 — The Planning Gate: Safety by Design

- **I needed something to stop me from moving too fast on the wrong things.**
- Three tiers: small task (just start), medium task (agree on approach first), large task (write a plan before anything is built).
- The large task gate is enforced. Not a guideline — enforced. The system won't let you build until the plan exists and is approved.
- Why this matters: the most expensive mistakes I made were from starting too fast on the wrong thing. This gate is the answer to that.
- It sounds annoying. In practice it takes 15 minutes and saves hours.

---

### Slide 15 — A Health Check for Your Workflow

- **Once a week: is the system working, or just running?**
- `mew doctor` checks everything in one command: active projects, learning streak, budget usage, open blockers.
- The most useful output: "No evidence in 30 days in Leadership." That's a nudge, not a guilt trip.
- The difference between a dashboard and a health check: a dashboard shows you state. A health check tells you what to do about it.
- I check this on Monday mornings. It sets the week.

## Act V — Your Turn

### Slide 16 — You Do Not Need to Build MewVault

- **Your one friction point is the starting point. Not the whole system.**
- This talk could make it sound like you need to build six interconnected workspaces to get value. You don't.
- Start with the thing that costs you the most invisible energy. Just that one thing.
- For me it was case studies. For you it might be meeting prep, or project handoffs, or feedback synthesis.
- The design brief writes itself once you name the friction.

---

### Slide 17 — What a Silo Actually Is

- **You already know how to design one. You've been doing it for other people.**
- A silo is just: one job to be done, rules for how the AI should behave, templates for recurring tasks, and a definition of done.
- That's a design spec. You write those for products every week.
- The only difference: this one is for you.
- Give a quick example: "A silo for meeting prep might mean: when I say 'prep for the Monday sync', pull my notes from last week, the agenda, and the open decisions. That's it. One job."

---

### Slide 18 — The Material Is New. The Method Is Not.

- **Design thinking still works. The material it's applied to has changed.**
- AI is a material. Like typography, colour, layout — it has properties you can work with. Constraints you design around. Behaviours you can predict and exploit.
- Empathise with yourself as the user. Define the friction. Prototype a rule. Test it for a week.
- That's design thinking. You've done it a hundred times for other people's problems.
- "The smallest version of the solution" — sound familiar? That's the design instinct you already have.

---

### Slide 19 — The Invitation

- **One rule. One friction point. 30 minutes.**
- Don't leave this as inspiration. Leave it as an experiment.
- Write it like a design decision: here is the problem, here is my proposed solution, here is how I will know if it works.
- Share it — with the team, in a Slack thread, wherever. We can critique it the same way we critique any other design work.
- Close strong: "Your workflow is a product. It deserves the same care you give everything else."

## The Silos in Practice

### Slide 20 — Divider: The Silos in Practice

- **Six focused workspaces. Each one started with a specific pain.**
- Brief pause. Transition energy: "Let me show you exactly what this looks like."
- You don't need to explain all six deeply. Pick 2-3 you feel most comfortable talking to.
- The silo slides are evidence, not new concepts. The concepts have already been introduced.

---

### Slide 21 — career-studio

- **Problem:** Reconstructing my own best work from memory at CV time. The real decisions were gone.
- **Solution:** Automatic session receipts → first draft assembled → voice pass → writing style learned.
- **Test:** Skill matrix — five pillars, evidence required for every level. Mock interviews from real history.
- _Talk to:_ the feeling of writing a weaker case study than the project deserved. That's the moment everyone in the room has felt.

---

### Slide 22 — design-studio

- **Problem:** Starting from zero every session. No paper trail between Figma decisions and outcomes.
- **Solution:** Figma is read directly (no transcription). Decisions saved with the frame that informed them. Session loads only what's relevant today.
- **Test:** One sync command flows every decision to the knowledge vault, linked to its Figma source.
- _Talk to:_ the experience of opening a Figma file three months later and having no idea why a component was built that way.

---

### Slide 23 — software-projects

- **Problem:** Features growing beyond what anyone agreed on. Expensive rework. Direction drift.
- **Solution:** Three tiers of planning. Large or risky tasks require a written plan — approved before anything is built.
- **Test:** Every large change has a traceable approved plan. Drifts are documented.
- _Talk to:_ the cost of building the wrong thing. This is a problem designers know well — the spec changes after the component is built.

---

### Slide 24 — idea-hub

- **Problem:** Good and bad ideas looked identical in a notes app. Easier to not look than to triage.
- **Solution:** Capture fast, filter early. Problem statement + user required before any research time is spent. Validation is structured — scan, compare, estimate, recommend.
- **Test:** Every idea has a visible lifecycle. Forward with evidence or archived with a reason.
- _Talk to:_ the graveyard of ideas that died not because they were bad, but because there was no system to tell the good ones from the bad ones.

---

### Slide 25 — game-lab

- **Problem:** Sessions getting bloated. "I think it works" was the progress metric. Too many changes at once.
- **Solution:** One change per step. Every step has a specific test. Errors stop everything. Next step only begins when the current one is confirmed.
- **Test:** Every mechanic and concept logged and counted. Session only closes when log and next step are defined.
- _Talk to:_ the creative chaos that feels productive but often isn't. Step-by-step discipline is the opposite of how we usually think about creative work — and that's the point.

---

### Slide 26 — learn-lab

- **Problem:** Daily practice needs consistency. AI is expensive and inconsistent for repetition. Scheduling logic kept drifting.
- **Solution:** Split the job. A simple script handles scheduling — deterministic, free. AI handles conversation, explanation, examples. Right tool for each job.
- **Test:** One command: reps done, accuracy, streak, tomorrow's due count. Computed locally. The number is honest.
- _Talk to:_ the broader principle — not everything needs AI. The best systems use the right tool for each part of the job. That's also a design instinct.

---

## General tips

- **Time:** 25-30 minutes with 5-10 minutes for discussion. Don't rush Act III — that's where the personal story lands.
- **If you lose the room:** skip to slide 16 (Your Turn). The concepts are set up; the invitation is the payoff.
- **Best question to end with:** "What's one moment in your week that costs you invisible energy?" Give the room 2 minutes to write it down before you open discussion.
- **The slides are yours.** If a story from a different project fits better, use it. Specificity wins over accuracy to the slide.
